Beginnings

Matthew (Maciej) Morycinski
Tuesday, January 17, 2023
Personal stories

Grandpa’s typewriter was my first writing instrument. This had many advantages: no need to learn to hold a pen; no risk of smearing; it held paper for me. Legibility of text was perfect, if the reader overlooked a few words that were X-ed out. Of course, I had to learn to read first! – at least well enough to be able to recognize characters shown on keys. I quickly learned that there were many ways to write the same word, and made sure to ask Mom for correct spelling.

I could never do it in English; we all know how chaotic and unpredictable English orthography is. Polish spelling is mostly phonetical, with few predictable exceptions. An occasional shout to Mom was enough for me to produce a mostly correct text.

As I grew, it quickly became apparent that my family gained a geek. My biggest childhood interests were:

  • Astronomy and astronautics, obviously due to the resounding success of the Apollo program. Illustrated articles about it were presented in shiny colour, unavailable to Polish publishers, on the pages of US embassy publication “America” that my Grandpa subscribed to. The impression it made on me was later a big factor in my dreams of life across the Atlantic.

  • Chemistry, mentored by my uncle’s friend, who was a geologist, as well as my grandpa the doctor. I got myself a little book by Stefan Sękowski, a chemistry popularizer par excellence. Soon I was trying to set up a home lab, bending glass tubing over a gas kitchen, and producing oxygen from potassium permanganate. I wasn’t into explosions; my passion was to understand how things work.

  • Aviation, as an airfield was nearby, and planes and helicopters flew frequently over our house. I made some airplane models from kits, with much help from my Dad, who was handy with such things. He also made sure I actually finished projects before starting new ones. If, as could be expected, I started yet another endeavour because I got bored with the previous one, he would say “Hayfire! You have to learn to finish!” Very true!

Later came a lifelong fascination with electronics and radio communications. One of family friends, Wojtek Zawistowski, was a computer engineer—mainframes of course, it was the ’70s—and a ham radio operator, as now-silent key SP5GM. He was legally blind, an inventor, and entrepreneur. He encouraged my interest in radio, and gave me many electronic parts, with which I constructed my first circuits. I soon used my uncle’s book of electronic projects for inspiration and advice. By the end of primary school, I have built a couple of things that were actually useful.

Books

I always loved science-fiction. My favourite writer was a famous Polish author Stanisław Lem. He is not as well known in English-speaking countries, but many of his books were translated into multiple languages. One of his novels, “Solaris,” was made into a movie, first in the USSR, and later in the USA.

Lem abandoned standard science-fiction genre quite early, as it was associated with pulp-quality fiction. He developed his own style, based on the idea of a civilization of robots, with distinct traits of a medieval culture. English translations, by the excellent translator Michael Kandel, use a style based on the writings of Chaucer, to convey a similar feeling. It evokes vague associations with steampunk.

Science and technology is there, but mostly as an excuse to tell excellent stories. They relate to Lem’s philosophical thoughts, as well as contemporary issues at the time of writing. Lem later said “People thought I was writing comedy, but really, I did because steam was coming out of my ears!” Example: a story about an orchestra where all instruments were broken, and once they were replaced, a monster came out of a cage and started eating the musicians. We all knew it was about communist politics.

Now for the anecdote

My family lived in a two-story row housing unit. As a toddler, I was usually let loose upstairs, with a wooden fence blocking my access to the staircase. At the top of the stairs stood our beautiful, big, Chinese copper vase. It was covered with cobalt blue laque, and had a design of slender flowers. I believe it was a pre-war antique, a big one – at least a metre high. One day, everyone was startled by horrible banging. My Mom almost had a heart attack, thinking I must have fallen down the stairs, but it was the vase. I managed to reach so far past the fence that I pushed it down. Apparently, I was born with some traits of a cat!

We kept that vase, so I became intimately familiar with its wounded and cracked nature, a reminder that things pushed off stairs do break, sometimes irreparably.

Trivia

  • My Mom was puzzled, and terrified, by my ability to crack glasses with my teeth while I was drinking from them. She quickly learned to keep a mustard glass handy for coffee shop outings.
  • Riding a bus with my Grandma was my introduction to reading, with help from neon signs. She read them out for me, and pretty soon I caught up. I could read books by the age of 5. I also had the obligatory letter blocks.

Family historical facts

  • My Mom’s great-grandfather on her father’s side, has set up the original Institute for Smallpox Vaccination in Warsaw, when Poland was still partitioned between Russia, Austria and Germany. It was a very successful family business.

  • My other great-grandfather—on maternal grandmother’s side—was Russian. He travelled to Russia during the 1917 Bolshevik Revolution, and subsequently died in one of Stalin’s purges.

  • My Mom’s father was the founder, and Editor-In-Chief, of a successful health education magazine Zdrowie (“Health” in Polish) that is still in print as of today.

  • My Dad, Grzegorz Moryciński, was a fine artist, a painter.

  • My Mom worked as a tour organizer for artistic groups - an impresario.
